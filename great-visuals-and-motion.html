The craft of great visuals
and great motion.

A field guide to why some designs and animations feel effortless and alive — and how to build that feeling yourself, one principle at a time. Every idea here is shown, not just told.

SCROLL TO BEGIN
Before we start

Great visual work looks like talent. It is almost always a small set of principles applied with restraint. The gap between amateur and professional is rarely imagination — it is knowing which levers exist, what each one does, and having the discipline to pull only the ones that matter.

This guide is in two halves that feed each other. Great visuals is the craft of the still frame: how you arrange, weight, space, and color things so the eye knows where to go and where to rest. Great motion is the craft of change over time: how things enter, move, and settle so they feel physical, purposeful, and alive. Motion sits on top of visuals — if a frozen frame of your animation looks flat, no amount of movement will save it. So we build the foundation first.

“The most important thing is to entertain the audience — to make them believe, to make them feel.”

— the spirit behind Frank Thomas & Ollie Johnston, Disney Animation: The Illusion of Life, the book that gave us the 12 principles.

Two ideas run underneath everything that follows, so hold them the whole way through. First: your job is to direct attention — guide the eye to what matters, and give it room to rest everywhere else. Second: every choice should mean something. A color, a movement, a gap that isn't carrying information is just noise, and noise is the enemy of clarity.

I

Part One

Great Visuals

The craft of the still frame — arrangement, weight, space, type, and color. Master this and every frame of your motion work already looks good before anything moves.

01 — Visual hierarchy

The eye reads in an order. You decide the order.

Before anyone reads a single word, they scan. Visual hierarchy is the art of making some things obviously more important than others, so the eye lands in the sequence you intend — first this, then that, then the detail. You build it with four tools: size, weight, color, and space. Bigger, bolder, higher-contrast, and more-isolated elements pull rank.

The classic test is the squint test: blur your design until you can't read it. If the most important thing is still the thing that jumps out, your hierarchy works. If everything turns to the same grey mush, you have a monotone — nothing leads.

02 — Gestalt principles

Your brain groups things automatically. Design with the grain.

In the 1920s, psychologists Wertheimer, Koffka, and Köhler showed that human perception doesn't see isolated dots — it organizes them into wholes, instantly and unconsciously. These Gestalt principles ("gestalt" = unified whole) are the physics of how people read a layout. Break them and users feel friction they can't name; use them and a design feels obvious.

01 Proximity

Things placed close together read as a group. Spacing, not boxes, is the cheapest way to show what belongs with what.

02 Similarity

Elements that share a color, shape, or size are read as related — even scattered across the page. Similarity groups; difference separates.

03 Closure

Given a few gaps, the mind completes the shape. You don't need every line — suggestion is enough, and often more elegant.

04 Continuity

The eye follows lines and curves. Elements arranged along a path feel connected and lead the gaze where you want it to travel.

05 Figure & ground

The eye separates a subject from its background. Contrast, blur, and elevation decide what reads as "on top" and grabs focus.

06 Common fate

Things that move together are read as one group — which is exactly why motion is such a powerful grouping and storytelling tool.

Watch “common fate” live: in the last tile, the purple dots drift together while the grey ones sit still — your eye instantly bundles the movers into a single unit. This is the bridge between visuals and motion: movement itself carries meaning.

03 — The four fundamentals

C.R.A.P. — the checklist that fixes most designs

In The Non-Designer's Design Book, Robin Williams distilled layout down to four principles with a memorable, slightly cheeky acronym. Run any design against them and you'll catch 80% of what makes it look amateur.

C Contrast

If two things aren't the same, make them very different — not slightly. Timid contrast (a headline only a touch bigger than body text) looks like a mistake. Bold contrast looks like a decision, and it creates hierarchy.

Aavs. a wall of same-size text

R Repetition

Repeat visual traits — a color, a corner radius, a spacing rhythm — throughout the work. Repetition is what makes a set of screens feel like one product rather than a pile of pages.

A Alignment

Nothing should be placed arbitrarily. Every element should line up with something else on an invisible edge. Jagged edges read as careless even when the viewer can't say why. Toggle the demo below.

P Proximity

Group related items; separate unrelated ones with space. Proximity turns a scattered list into organized chunks the eye can parse at a glance — the same Gestalt law, applied on purpose.

04 — Typography

Type is the voice. Set it well and everything sounds better.

Ellen Lupton's Thinking with Type and every serious type book agree on the load-bearing choices. Get these four right and your work reads as professional before anyone judges the words.

A clear type scale. Don't pick sizes at random — build a scale where each step is a deliberate jump (a common ratio is ~1.25–1.5×). Then use only a few of its steps. Restraint in sizes is what creates rhythm.

Contrast in weight, not just size

A bold 20px can outrank a regular 24px. Weight is a hierarchy tool people forget — and it's often more elegant than making things bigger.

Mind the measure

Line length matters more than font choice. Too wide and the eye loses its place returning to the next line; ~66 characters is the classic sweet spot.

Pair with contrast, not similarity

When mixing two typefaces, make them clearly different (a serif display + a sans body). Two similar fonts look like a mistake; a clear pairing looks intentional.

Spacing is typography too. Generous line-height (around 1.5 for body) and space between paragraphs do as much for readability as the font itself. Cramped text feels stressful; text that breathes feels trustworthy.

05 — Color

Everyone thinks color is taste. It's mostly discipline.

Josef Albers spent a career (Interaction of Color) showing that color is relational and slippery — a hue changes character depending on what surrounds it. The practical takeaways, though, are surprisingly mechanical, and two of them do most of the work. The card below is driven by a single accent hue: drag it, then hit the two tests.

Value beats hue

A design must work in greyscale first. Value (light-to-dark) carries the structure; hue is flavor on top. Hit “Grayscale test” — if it still reads, your values are right. Design in grey, add color last.

Pull saturation down

Fully-saturated color looks cheap and vibrates against white. Muted, slightly-desaturated color reads as considered and expensive. Most of “good taste” in color is just backing off the saturation.

The 60 / 30 / 10 rule

Roughly 60% dominant neutral, 30% secondary surface, 10% accent. The accent works because it's scarce. Flood the frame with it and it stops pointing at anything.

Let color mean something

Green/red for up/down, one accent for “interactive,” warm vs. cool for mood. Decorative color is noise; coded color is information.

One accent plus a neutral ramp is a complete, professional palette. You almost never need more — and a second accent should earn its place by signalling a genuinely different category.

06 — Space & composition

Empty space is not wasted space. It's the design's oxygen.

The single fastest way to make work look more expensive is to add whitespace. Crowding is the number-one amateur tell; beginners fear empty space and pros protect it. Space creates focus, groups content (Gestalt proximity again), and gives the eye somewhere to rest between ideas.

At the scale of a whole frame — a title card, a hero, a video shot — this becomes composition: deciding where things sit inside the rectangle.

Rule of thirds

Place focal points on the grid intersections, not dead center. Off-center is energy; dead-center is stable but inert (right for a logo, flat for a story).

Negative space

A large quiet emptiness gives the subject weight and reads as confident and cinematic. When a shot feels cheap, add more emptiness, not more stuff.

Balance without symmetry

A big element on one side balanced by a small one on the other (a seesaw) is stable and dynamic. Perfect symmetry is stable but static.

Grids & leading room

A grid (Müller-Brockmann's gift) gives everything a reason to sit where it sits. And leave space in front of anything that faces or moves in a direction.

07 — Depth & elevation

A believable shadow lifts an element into its own layer.

Depth tells the eye what's on top and what's behind — instant figure-and-ground. The trap is overdoing it. One soft, low-opacity shadow reads as an object resting above the surface; a heavy black drop-shadow reads as a beginner. Same lesson as everywhere: subtle reads as confident. Hover the cards.

Flat

Border only. Sits in the page. Good for dense UI where everything is equal.

Raised

One soft shadow. Lifts gently — the sweet spot for cards and buttons.

Floating

Bigger, softer shadow for modals and menus that sit above everything.

The rule of thumb for shadows: the higher an element floats, the larger and softer (not darker) the shadow, and the more it offsets downward — because light comes from above. Keep opacity low. Refactoring UI calls this building an “elevation system,” and it's what makes an interface feel physical.

II

Part Two

Great Motion

The craft of change over time — how things enter, move, and settle so they feel physical, purposeful, and alive. Motion is a spotlight: it only works when you point it at the right thing.

08 — The foundation

The 12 Principles of Animation

In 1981, Disney veterans Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston published The Illusion of Life and codified twelve principles their studio had discovered over decades — from Snow White onward. They were written for pencil animators, but every one still governs great motion today, from Pixar films to the button you just tapped. Many are animated live below.

01 Squash & Stretch

Objects deform as they move — squashing on impact, stretching in flight — which conveys weight and flexibility. The trick: keep the volume constant.

02 Anticipation

A small opposite move before the main action — a crouch before a jump. It prepares the eye so the action reads instead of surprising.

03 Staging

Direct attention to what matters in the frame. One clear idea at a time — pose, lighting, and placement so the point is unmistakable.

04 Straight-ahead & Pose-to-pose

Two workflows: draw frame-by-frame for fluid chaos (fire, water), or set key poses first and fill between them for control. Most work blends both.

05 Follow-through & Overlap

Parts don't stop all at once. When a body halts, hair and cloth keep going and settle after. Overlapping timing is what kills the "robot" feel.

06 Slow In & Slow Out

Real things accelerate and decelerate — they don't start at full speed. This is easing, the single biggest tell of pro vs. amateur motion. (Top eases; faint one is linear.)

07 Arcs

Nature moves along curved paths, not straight lines. Arcing motion looks organic; perfectly straight motion looks mechanical and dead.

08 Secondary Action

A supporting motion that enriches the main one — a facial expression during a walk. It adds life as long as it doesn't distract from the primary action.

09 Timing

The number of frames a move takes sets its weight and mood. Fast = light and snappy; slow = heavy and grand. Timing is acting.

10 Exaggeration

Push reality further than literal truth for clarity and impact. A little overshoot, a bigger reaction — caricature reads better than accuracy.

11 Solid Drawing

Respect volume, weight, and three-dimensional space. Even flat/stylized work needs a believable sense of form and balance underneath it.

12 Appeal

The charisma of a design — clear shapes, pleasing proportion, personality. Not "pretty," but compelling: something the eye wants to keep watching.

09 — Easing

The one lever that jumps your work a full tier

Of everything here, easing is the highest-leverage single skill. It's "slow in & slow out" from the 12 principles, expressed as a curve: how an object's speed changes over the course of a move. Linear motion — constant speed — is the loudest "beginner" signal there is, because nothing in the physical world moves at a constant speed. The playground runs the same move twice: grey is linear, purple uses the curve you pick. Watch the curve at the bottom — its shape is the feeling.

Steeper = faster at that moment. Ease-out starts steep and flattens (fast, then settles). Overshoot & spring cross above the finish and come back — that's what reads as playful and alive.

The working rule: elements entering and settling use ease-out (fast then slow — the workhorse for ~90% of UI and explainer motion). Elements exiting use ease-in (slow then fast — they accelerate away). Overshoot/spring is for personality and emphasis. Linear is almost only right for continuous loops like a spinner. And use custom curves, not the CSS defaults — Val Head notes that a signature curve is part of a brand's motion identity.

10 — Timing & duration

How long a thing takes is how heavy it feels

Timing is acting. The same move at different speeds tells a different story — fast reads as light and snappy, slow reads as heavy and deliberate. In interfaces there's a usable range that's been measured to death, and Google's Material Design codified it.

100–200ms

Micro-interactions. Hovers, toggles, button feedback. Fast enough to feel instant and responsive — the reply to a direct action.

200–300ms

The standard. Material's reference duration for most transitions — cards expanding, elements entering. The comfortable default.

300–500ms

Bigger moves. Full-screen or inter-screen transitions, larger objects. Big things should move slower — it reads as mass.

Two quiet rules that matter: larger objects and longer distances need more time (mass). And exits can be a touch faster than entrances — you invite things in gently, but let them leave briskly so they don't linger and block the user.

11 — Staging & choreography

Stagger the entrance and the scene narrates itself

If easing is how one thing moves, staging is what moves when. Revealing everything at once gives the eye nowhere to land. A stagger — elements entering in sequence — creates a reading order and lets complexity assemble one piece at a time. This is the biggest reason good explainer videos feel "directed." Direction should mean something, too. Steps that stack downward should rise in order; something arriving from off-screen-left implies it "comes from earlier in the flow." Never animate direction at random. And remember composition here: elements should enter from the negative space and settle into their resting positions — the layout tells you where entrances come from.

12 — Motion with purpose

In interfaces, motion is a tool — never decoration

Character animation entertains; interface animation informs. Material Design frames good UI motion with three jobs, and every animation you add should be doing at least one of them — if it isn't, cut it.

Informative

Motion shows relationships and cause-and-effect. A menu that grows out of the button it belongs to tells you where it came from and where it'll return.

Focused

Motion directs attention to what matters right now — and nowhere else. It guides the eye through a change instead of letting things blink in and out.

Expressive

Motion adds personality and moments of delight, and reinforces the brand — sparingly, and never at the cost of the first two jobs.

Preserve continuity

When something transforms, animate it so the user can follow the same object through the change (Rachel Nabors calls this using motion to maintain context). Cutting hard makes people re-orient; a smooth transition keeps them oriented.

Respect the reflex

Honor prefers-reduced-motion. Big parallax and spins can trigger nausea and vestibular issues for real users. Great motion includes a graceful, calmer fallback — this very page has one.

13 — Restraint & secondary motion

Subtract until only the meaningful thing moves — then add life back there

These two pull in opposite directions, which is the point. Restraint decides what moves: if everything animates, nothing is emphasized, because a spotlight only works in the dark. The mechanics of a good secondary beat: it's delayed ~100–150ms behind the primary move, and it uses a slight overshoot (the Easing.back / spring curve) so it reads as having mass. In code this is just an offset: if the card animates over frames 0–15, the badge animates over 6–22 — the same idea, shifted a few frames later.

The reusable recipe

That beat generalizes to almost any explainer moment. Internalize this sequence and your work will look authored:

1Compose the frame — focal point on the thirds, negative space protected.
2Reveal staggered — elements enter in reading order, ~100–150ms apart.
3Ease everything out — rise + fade with an ease-out curve; nothing snaps.
4Hold everything still — only the one thing you're explaining moves.
5One secondary beat — a small overshoot pop at the payoff moment.
6One accent color — marks what matters; everything else neutral.
